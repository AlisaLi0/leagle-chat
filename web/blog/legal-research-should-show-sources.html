Legal Research Should Show Its Sources

Why JuriCodex is built as a source-grounded legal research workspace, not an AI lawyer.

Published by JuriCodex · U.S. legal research · Source-grounded answers

A legal answer that sounds plausible is not enough.

In law, the source matters. A case citation matters. The exact quoted language matters. The jurisdiction, posture, and limits of an authority matter. If an AI system gives a confident legal-sounding answer without showing where it came from, the user is left with the worst possible combination: fluency without accountability.

That is the problem I wanted to work on with JuriCodex.

JuriCodex is a legal research workspace for U.S. law. It is not an AI lawyer, and it does not provide legal advice. The goal is narrower and, I think, more useful: help people find real legal authorities, inspect the citations, verify quoted language, and understand what the sources actually say.

Why source-first matters

Most legal questions do not turn on a single sentence. They depend on facts, jurisdiction, procedural posture, statutes, regulations, and lines of cases that may point in different directions.

A generic AI answer can be helpful as a starting point, but it can also hide the hard part: whether the answer is actually grounded in real law.

For legal research, I think the workflow should be reversed:

  1. Find the relevant authorities.
  2. Show the user what was found.
  3. Let the user open the source.
  4. Verify the quoted language.
  5. Only then organize an answer around the sources.

That is the design principle behind JuriCodex.

What JuriCodex does

JuriCodex lets users ask legal research questions in plain English, then searches for relevant U.S. legal authorities and organizes the results around the sources.

The current version includes:

  • source-grounded research chat
  • case search by concept, keyword, case name, or citation
  • laws and rules search
  • citation extraction from pasted legal text
  • case resolver
  • brief review
  • quote verification against source text
  • case details and source links
  • table-of-authorities style output
  • research history and export

The important part is not that an answer is generated. The important part is that the answer points back to materials the user can inspect.

A research tool, not legal advice.
JuriCodex helps users find and inspect legal authorities. It does not replace a licensed attorney, and users should verify sources before relying on them.

What JuriCodex does not do

JuriCodex does not tell users whether they will win or lose a case.

It does not replace a licensed attorney.

It does not guarantee that a legal question has been fully resolved.

That boundary is intentional. In legal research, overconfidence is a product risk.

Examples

A user might ask:

  • Can my landlord keep my security deposit for normal wear and tear?
  • Can I be fired after reporting workplace safety violations?
  • What did Miranda v. Arizona actually hold?
  • What are the Fourth Amendment limits on warrantless car searches?

For each question, the goal is not to produce a magic answer. The goal is to find relevant authorities, show the citations, surface the limits of the retrieved materials, and make the research path visible.

Who this is for

JuriCodex is built for people who need to understand legal authority but may not have access to expensive legal research platforms:

  • pro se litigants
  • legal aid workers
  • students
  • researchers
  • small firms
  • journalists
  • anyone trying to understand what U.S. legal sources actually say

What I am still working on

There is a lot still to improve.

Legal research quality depends heavily on retrieval quality. Citation treatment is hard. Jurisdiction matters. Statutes and regulations need better structured handling. Some source text is incomplete or messy. Quote verification is useful, but it does not replace a full good-law check.

I am also working on better research history, better source ranking, better brief review, and clearer ways to show when an answer depends on facts the user has not provided.

The long-term goal is not to make a chatbot that sounds like a lawyer. The goal is to build a research workspace where the sources remain visible.
